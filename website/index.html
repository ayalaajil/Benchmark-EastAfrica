Verdict: GenCast is the strongest system on every axis, but its ensemble is overconfident (finding 4 below), and no model reliably beats climatology outside the equatorial belt.

1. GenCast is the strongest overall

It has the lowest CRPS (~2.0 mm day⁻¹), the highest and most stable anomaly correlation, the best-calibrated exceedance probabilities, and is the only model that beats climatology, over the equatorial belt.

Blue = beats the CHIRPS climatology baseline (CRPSS > 0). GenCast holds a blue equatorial band out to lead day 7; GraphCast and FourCastNet are mostly red (worse than climatology). Full set: the Skill vs climatology tab.

2. All models have limited deterministic skill

Anomaly correlation stays below the 0.6 "useful-skill" guide at every lead, and degrades with lead time for two of the three models — these are genuinely hard, convection-dominated rains.

No model reaches the ACC = 0.6 guide at any lead. GenCast (≈0.36–0.41) is highest and flattest; GraphCast degrades fastest, from ≈0.28 to ≈0.09 by day 7. Full set: the Deterministic tab.

3. Distinct, stable bias signatures

FourCastNet is systematically dry (≈ −1.2 mm day⁻¹); GraphCast drifts wet at longer leads (+0.84 mm day⁻¹ by day 7); GenCast is nearly unbiased in the domain mean.

Seven-day rolling bias (top) and MAE (bottom) vs valid date, one column per lead day. The three models keep their sign and character throughout the season. Full set: the Deterministic tab.

4. GenCast is under-dispersive (overconfident)

Spread–skill ratio rises from ~0.5 (day 1) toward ~0.8 (days 5–7) but never reaches the calibrated SSR = 1 line, and rank histograms are U-shaped — the ensemble is too narrow, especially at short lead.

Left: SSR vs lead, with the under-dispersive region (SSR < 1) shaded. Right: the two ingredients (RMSE, spread) plotted separately. Full set: the Probabilistic tab.

5. Results are observation-sensitive

Skill and calibration shift visibly across CHIRPS / ERA5 / TAMSAT, underscoring observational uncertainty over this data-sparse region.

Against CHIRPS and TAMSAT the rank histograms are sharply U-shaped (rank 0 ≈ 0.47–0.55); against ERA5 they are far flatter (rank 0 ≈ 0.19) — GenCast looks much better calibrated against the reanalysis it was partly trained near than against the independent gauge–satellite products. Full set: the Probabilistic tab.

What this is

A self-contained, reproducible benchmark of machine-learning weather models on daily total rainfall over East Africa (12°S–15°N, 28°E–52°E, 1° grid, land cells only). Every figure and table on this site is produced by one command, run_verification.py, from publicly available inputs.

Protocol — MAM 2024

Each model is initialized daily at 00 UTC from ERA5 analyses over the March–May 2024 long-rains season (92 initializations, 1 Mar – 31 May) and verified at lead days 1, 3, 5 and 7: a forecast started on day t is scored against observations valid on t+ℓ. Daily totals are formed on each model's native cadence, then conservatively regridded to the common 1° grid (mm day⁻¹).

References

Every model is verified against three independent rainfall products — CHIRPS v2.0 (primary, gauge–satellite), ERA5 (reanalysis) and TAMSAT v3.1 (satellite) — so conclusions are not artifacts of a single observational product.

Data & downloads

Every figure on this site is written twice by the pipeline — a 300-dpi PNG (shown here) and a vector PDF (the ⬇ PDF button on each card, for papers). Every table on the Tables tab has a ⬇ CSV download. The full numeric results are written by run_verification.py to mam2024_analysis_outputs/; they are not committed to the repository (they are regenerated from the prediction Zarr stores), but every one is reproducible with the single command below.

Reproducibility

Everything is regenerated by two commands — inference, then verification. All inputs are public: model checkpoints and ERA5 initial conditions are read from anonymous Google Cloud Storage, and CHIRPS/ERA5/TAMSAT are downloaded and cached on first use. Inference needs a GPU and the conda environments under envs/. 

# 1. Inference — GenCast / GraphCast / FourCastNet / climatology, MAM 2024
./run_inference.sh --models gencast graphcast fourcastnet climatology \
    --start 2024-03-01 --end 2024-05-31 --lead-days 1 3 5 7

# NeuralGCM (own env; ensemble size and variables via env vars)
./run_neuralgcm.sh

# 2. Verification — writes every figure (PDF + PNG) and CSV table
python run_verification.py --pred-dir data/predictions \
    --start 2024-03-01 --end 2024-05-31 \
    --models fourcastnet gencast graphcast neuralgcm

# 3. Tests
pytest tests/ -q

Complete prediction files are skipped on rerun, so inference is resumable; pass --overwrite to force regeneration. If data/predictions/climatology/ exists, the climatology baseline is loaded automatically and the CRPS skill score (table + maps) is produced.

Glossary

Data products

MAMMarch–April–May — the "long rains" flood season of the Greater Horn of Africa; the 2024 season is the benchmark's evaluation period.
CHIRPSClimate Hazards center Infrared Precipitation with Stations — a blended gauge–satellite rainfall product (0.05°); the primary observational reference here.
ERA5ECMWF's fifth-generation atmospheric reanalysis; used both as model initial conditions and as a secondary rainfall reference.
TAMSATTropical Applications of Meteorology using SATellite data — a satellite-only rainfall estimate (0.0375°); a third, independent reference.
Lead dayDays between forecast initialization and the valid date being verified (1, 3, 5, 7 in this benchmark).
Init(ialization)The date/time a forecast run starts from — MAM 2024 uses 92 daily initializations (1 Mar – 31 May).

Deterministic accuracy

BiasMean (forecast − observation); positive = wet bias, negative = dry bias.
MAEMean absolute error — average magnitude of the error, ignoring sign.
RMSERoot-mean-square error — like MAE but penalizes large errors more heavily.
ACCAnomaly correlation coefficient — spatial correlation between forecast and observed anomalies versus lead time. Values above ~0.6 are conventionally considered "useful skill."

Probabilistic / ensemble

CRPSContinuous Ranked Probability Score — a proper score comparing a full forecast distribution to the observation; lower is better. This site uses the fair CRPS (Ferro 2014), an unbiased estimator for finite ensembles.
CRPSSCRPS Skill Score, 1 − CRPSmodel / CRPSreference. Positive means the model beats the reference (here, climatology); zero is break-even.
SpreadThe ensemble's own standard deviation — how much disagreement exists among members.
SSRSpread–skill ratio: ensemble spread ÷ ensemble-mean RMSE. SSR = 1 means the stated uncertainty matches the actual error; SSR < 1 means under-dispersive / overconfident.
Rank histogramHistogram of the observation's rank within the sorted ensemble, pooled over forecasts (tie-aware, Hamill 2001). Flat = calibrated; U-shaped = under-dispersive; dome = over-dispersive.
Reliability diagramForecast probability (of exceeding a threshold) vs observed frequency; a calibrated forecast lies on the diagonal.
ECEExpected Calibration Error — average gap between forecast probability and observed frequency in a reliability diagram. Lower is better.

Event-based

PODProbability of detection — fraction of observed events correctly forecast. Higher is better.
FARFalse-alarm ratio — fraction of forecast events that did not occur. Lower is better.
CSICritical success index — combines hits, misses and false alarms; balances POD and FAR. Higher is better.
Frequency biasRatio of forecast to observed event counts. 1 is unbiased; > 1 over-forecasts, < 1 under-forecasts.
Brier scoreMean squared error of a probability forecast against the binary outcome. Lower is better (Murphy 1973 decomposition).
BSSBrier skill score — Brier score as skill relative to a reference forecast, analogous to CRPSS.
